# First Light Terraces

First Light occupies five broad stone steps above a hooked harbor. The lowest two are working surfaces rather than permanent neighborhoods: ramps, scoring racks, brine troughs, and numbered bays can be cleared before the Ember Tide stiffens. Houses climb above them in narrow courts whose roofs throw afternoon shade onto the next morning's harvesting water.

The settlement's day is divided by safe access, not by a civic clock. Before dawn, quay stewards mark warm seams and households pull shutters across the lowest lanes. Ash-divers work the skin while carriers move plates uphill. When the glass begins to craze, a bell made from ordinary kiln glass ends the harvest even if valuable sheets remain. Ignoring that bell is treated as endangering carriers, not as admirable daring.

Most plates serve modest purposes: checking heat loss, lining food boxes, or making single-cycle workshop gauges. The settlement exports selected sheets during the Pale Current season, but their rapid decay limits distance and price. Grain and salt arriving on that current matter more to survival than glass prestige. Storage courts fill quickly, so a good route season produces arguments over warehouse shade rather than effortless prosperity.

First Light has no unified governing order. Bay stewards control access to individual ramps, households maintain cooling houses by neighborhood compact, and visiting pilots negotiate seasonal marks. These arrangements overlap and sometimes delay one another. None reaches the inland Rain Vault, and residents do not assume the archive shares their tidal customs.
