# Ash-Divers

Ash-divers work at arm's depth along the edge of a forming Ember Tide. They read the skin through ripples: matte red means a plate can be lifted, silver sparks indicate a seam still hot enough to blister through wet gloves, and a dark line warns that the whole sheet is about to fold. Crews use hooked bone, soaked fiber, and ordinary wooden frames because metal carries the remembered heat too quickly.

Skill is taught by crew and can be left. It is not inherited, although households with spare labor and cooling-house standing can support longer apprenticeships. Pay comes by intact frame after deductions for shared tools. Because glass value falls within days, a missed tide cannot be repaid by hoarding later harvests.

The occupational limit is cumulative tremor and loss of heat sense. Cooling houses keep exposure ledgers so crews can rotate people before a hand fails over live glass. This protection also removes wages, which is why some divers hide numbness or work with visiting crews outside their district. Veterans often become seam callers or frame repairers rather than romantic masters immune to harm.

Their drowned-caldera origin account is respected but disputed. It grows from work observations—deep bands feel different under a hook—rather than privileged access to cosmic truth.
