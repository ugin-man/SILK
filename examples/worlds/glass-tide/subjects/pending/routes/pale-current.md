# Pale Current

The Pale Current is a cool freshwater lens driven along the outer coast after upland melt and steady crosswinds. Fine chalk makes its surface look milk-blue from a mast. For six to nine weeks, shallow vessels can ride its edge toward First Light with predictable leeway. Outside that season, contrary chop and uncertain water make the same passage slow and expensive.

The route carries grain, salt, new apprentices, letters, and replacement timber. It does not preserve Ember glass; sheets decay at the same rate aboard a current-bound vessel. This constraint keeps glass trade local while allowing harvesting tools and knowledge to travel. First Light must store staples in a brief rush and then endure long informational gaps.

Pilots renew stone and float marks each season. When they leave, storms scatter the floats and the route becomes knowledge rather than infrastructure. No institution owns the current, and no navigator can summon it. Its cause—meltwater, chalk, and wind—is independent of tidal heat memory.
