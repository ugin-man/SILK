# Ember Tide

The Ember Tide is a coastal return in which the upper water releases heat it absorbed from sun-warmed rock, smoke, kilns, and living bodies during the previous day. Near dawn, a red-black film forms over sheltered reaches. It stiffens into glass from the top down, then crazes and sinks within about forty minutes. The heat is remembered unevenly: a shaded quay produces dull, cool plates while a fire-scorched wall may print a dangerous bright seam across the next tide.

The glass is useful because it holds a trace of that heat for one more tidal cycle. It can warm a wrapped meal, reveal where a wall leaked heat overnight, or be annealed into small heat-marking tools. It cannot be stockpiled as power. Removed sheets begin losing their pattern immediately; reheating them erases rather than renews the memory; large stacks crack inward and ruin adjacent plates. Attempts to bank a season of heat produce heavy, ordinary cullet.

Harvesters therefore alter the phenomenon without controlling it. Score lines left by ash-divers guide later fractures, while too many cuts turn a safe skin into needle-like waste. First Light closes its lowest steps during the workable interval and treats missed windows as lost material, not as a crisis that can be solved by stronger magic.

No origin is settled. Deep-water bands support the drowned-caldera account, but old coastal kiln slag can reproduce similar marks. The tide's observable cycle is canon; the story of what first taught water to retain heat remains a disputed claim.
