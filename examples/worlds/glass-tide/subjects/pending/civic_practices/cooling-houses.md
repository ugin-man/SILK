# Cooling Houses

Each First Light neighborhood keeps a low room behind its deepest shared shade. Its shelves hold cool brine jars, clean wrapping cloth, hand splints, and burn ledgers. The house does not cure heat injury. It buys time, makes exposure visible, and gives an injured worker somewhere to be watched until swelling or tremor declares whether ordinary rest is enough.

The practice began after the Three-Quay Fire, when crews carried the burned uphill only to find every household guarding its last cool water. The later compact requires each household to provide shade cloth, brine, or watch hours during harvest season. On the first safe evening, neighbors tie the year's donated cloth into a long canopy and eat beneath it before the fabric is cut for service. The custom is attractive because useful preparation becomes a public meal, not because the community has abolished scarcity.

Authority stays narrow. A house attendant may record that a diver should miss the next tide, but cannot arrest them or command another district. Households can fall into arrears, visiting crews may be refused non-emergency supplies, and a long hot spell can exhaust brine. Appeals are settled by neighboring households that contributed the disputed stock. No central board controls all cooling houses, and inland settlements need not have them.
