# System design lens

Use for magic, supernatural capabilities, technologies, legal mechanisms, economic systems, communication systems, or other rule-bearing structures.

## System promise

State what experience and world function the system should create. A system may support wonder, tactical clarity, mystery, social hierarchy, horror, craft, ritual, convenience, or another purpose. Do not maximize complexity without purpose.

## Competing ontologies

Before fixing rules, compare possibilities:

- one underlying reality with several cultural models;
- several overlapping mechanisms;
- unrelated phenomena grouped by language;
- a reliable practice built on a false theory;
- partially knowable or perspective-dependent operation;
- intentional mystery with stable consequences.

## Primitive operations

Identify what changes at the smallest meaningful level. Attributes and spell names are not primitives unless the world makes them so. Consider transformation, transfer, binding, separation, perception, probability, relation, permission, memory, identity, location, time, life, matter, or world-specific operations.

## Composition

Ask how operations combine, interfere, sequence, amplify, cancel, distort, or refuse combination. Higher difficulty need not mean more attributes. It may arise from precision, simultaneous constraints, scale, distance, interpretation, coordination, risk, permission, or irreversible commitment.

## Access

Distinguish ability, knowledge, permission, material access, bodily compatibility, social recognition, inheritance, relationship, location, and survival. A being may produce a phenomenon without using the human system that classifies it.

## Capability boundary

Define what the system can do, cannot do, appears able to do, and has never been tested doing. Limits may be ontological, practical, institutional, ethical, informational, geographic, temporal, or unknown. Do not require a payment cost when another boundary better fits.

## Failure and misuse

Inspect misunderstanding, partial success, hidden side effects, scaling failure, coordination failure, bad inputs, hostile conditions, exploitation, sabotage, counterfeit practice, and institutional misuse. Do not make every failure spectacular.

## Taxonomies and ranks

If attributes, levels, schools, or ranks exist, identify who created them, what they measure, where they fail, who rejects them, and whether they match reality. A school curriculum can be socially real without being an ontological truth.

## Nonhuman modes

For divine, draconic, fae, ancestral, artificial, collective, or alien phenomena, decide whether they share mechanism, access the same mechanism differently, or are misclassified as the same thing. Avoid making every mode a stronger tier of human practice.

## Historical paradigms

Track discoveries, lost practices, reforms, competing theories, standardized teaching, suppressed models, regional schools, technological substitution, and unexplained cases. New theory should change practice or interpretation.

## Scale stress

Apply `simulation-and-consistency.md` to individual, repeated, coordinated, institutional, adversarial, and infrastructural use. Record accepted consequences or scope contraction.

## Exit evidence

A load-bearing system is deep enough when readers can understand its intended experience, operating possibilities, access, limits, meaningful variation, historical development, institutional and lived consequences, high-risk interactions, and unresolved edges without relying on a decorative classification table.
