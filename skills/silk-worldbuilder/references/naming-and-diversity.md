# Naming and expressive diversity

## Name after understanding

Decide the subject's role, intended appeal, naming community, history, users, opponents, and register before choosing a final name. Internally compare alternatives and commit to one canonical name. Keep aliases only when actual speakers use them differently.

The canonical `title` is a reader-facing label for navigation, not necessarily an in-world proper name. A nameless, forbidden-name, contested, translated, or radically nonhuman subject may use a clear editorial label while the body explains how inhabitants refer to or avoid it. Do not invent an in-world name merely to satisfy metadata.

## Naming ecology

Inspect the world-wide mixture of:

- transparent common terms;
- proper name plus common class;
- inherited historical names;
- translated names and exonyms;
- offices and titles;
- nicknames, insults, and shortened forms;
- ceremonial forms;
- opaque coined names.

No fixed quota applies. Avoid uniform character counts, repeated suffixes, premise-word saturation, and long runs of unfamiliar coinages. Do not ban plain names; ordinary names make extraordinary ones legible.

## Avoid explanatory overreach

A world where magic is ordinary does not put `magic` in every institution name, just as a computer-using administration is not called a computer administration bureau. Name from institutional history, jurisdiction, founder, location, legal inheritance, public usage, or political compromise when appropriate.

## Test names in use

Check pronunciation, shortening, misunderstanding, avoidance, mockery, translation, bureaucratic preservation, and differences between official and local use. A name should work in the mouths of inhabitants, not only in a lore list.

## Separate naming from structural review

Removing names can expose accidental templates, but familiarity after name removal does not make a subject uninteresting. Use blind comparison to detect interchangeability, then judge intended appeal and world fit separately.

## Finality

Do not leave four or five unresolved name candidates inside approved canon. Select one. Keep alternatives only as meaningful aliases, disputed names, historical names, or exonyms.
