# Cosmology and nonhuman lens

## Avoid human defaults

Do not assume every deity, spirit, dragon, collective mind, artifact intelligence, cosmic entity, or alien ecology has human individuality, motives, family, government, language, territory, morality, worship needs, or power hierarchy.

Ask what kind of identity, persistence, perception, agency, memory, relation, and change are possible for the subject.

## Cosmological layers

Possible concerns include creation accounts, world structure, afterlife, dream, time, causality, divinity, fate, soul, matter, void, and boundaries between modes of existence. Treat them as hypotheses selected by the world, not a required checklist.

## Account versus truth

Separate cosmological reality from myths, doctrines, visions, magical models, and institutional claims. Several accounts may remain viable. Use `epistemics-and-mystery.md` rather than choosing a complete answer solely for neatness.

## Nonhuman cognition

Consider differences in scale, time perception, embodiment, memory, attention, reproduction, death, communication, collectivity, and relation to place. Show consequences without making difference incomprehensible by decorative prose alone.

## Power and limitation

Power need not behave like spells or statistics. Define observable reach, modes of action, constraints, indifference, incompatibilities, and evidence. Do not require every powerful being to have a simple exploitable weakness.

## Worship and relation

A transcendent subject may be worshiped, ignored, exploited, misidentified, negotiated with, embodied in practice, or unknown. Worship does not automatically create clergy, temples, domains, commandments, or a universal religion.

## Cosmic horror

Preserve disorientation, scale mismatch, incomplete knowledge, and consequences when those are intended appeals. Do not explain away horror by producing a total taxonomy. Depth may exist around an unresolved center.

## Exit evidence

Cosmology or a nonhuman subject is deep enough when its mode of existence, observable consequences, relation to knowledge, meaningful limits, historical interpretations, and effects on other subjects are developed to the intended experience without being forced into human or game-system defaults.
