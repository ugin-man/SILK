# Culture and language lens

## Culture is not a trait list

Develop practices through material conditions, history, contact, institutions, identity, transmission, internal disagreement, and ordinary enactment. Do not assign one personality, value, costume, food, or speech pattern to an entire people or species.

## Internal variation

Inspect region, class, profession, generation, religion, gender or kinship role, migration, urban and rural differences, and other world-relevant divisions. Do not mechanically include every familiar axis.

## Transmission and change

Ask how practices and identities are learned, enforced, improvised, forgotten, revived, borrowed, translated, commodified, or rejected. Shared culture needs contact or shared conditions.

## Language ecology

When language matters, inspect multilingualism, dialect continua, prestige, literacy, scripts, translation, trade languages, ritual registers, naming, taboo, language shift, and misunderstanding. Do not create one language per nation or species by default.

## Names in context

Use `naming-and-diversity.md`. Derive naming patterns from actual communities and history, but preserve ordinary variation and contact. Do not encode every cultural fact into every proper noun.

## Identity and outsiders

Separate self-identification, official categories, outsider names, historical labels, and contested membership. A category used by a state may not match lived identity.

## Practice and meaning

The same practice can have several meanings and an old meaning can survive after belief changes. Avoid making all rituals direct expressions of one metaphysical truth.

## Cultural contact

Inspect borrowing, resistance, hybrid practice, unequal exchange, translation errors, diaspora, and border conditions where relevant. Do not isolate cultures into sealed themed boxes.

## Exit evidence

A culture or language system is deep enough when readers can see variation, transmission, historical change, material practice, identity disputes, and contact without reducing people to an encyclopedia stereotype.
