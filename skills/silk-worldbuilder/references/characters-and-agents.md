# Characters and agents lens

## Role in V1

Create a person or agent when their choices, position, relationships, memory, work, authority, or historical consequences are independently important to the world. Do not create one named representative for every institution or culture merely to humanize a list.

## Canonical depth

Develop relevant identity, background, current position, knowledge, beliefs, capabilities, limitations, obligations, relationships, material circumstances, prior decisions, reputation, and current pressures.

Do not reduce a person to faction function, species stereotype, trauma, secret, combat ability, or future plot purpose.

## Agency without arc

Record what the agent wants, can attempt, cannot control, misunderstands, and might plausibly change. Preserve several possible developments. Do not select a destined transformation, victory, defeat, romance, quest, scene sequence, or ending.

## Historical figures

Separate the person from events, institutions, legends, and later uses of their memory when those have independent identity. Keep a summary of those consequences in the person's canonical file.

## Nonhuman and collective agents

Do not force human psychology, individuality, lifespan, family, or communication onto deities, collective minds, institutions, artificial persons, or ecological agents. Route to `cosmology-and-nonhuman.md` where needed.

## Representative lives

To demonstrate lived consequences, a concise unnamed or minor example can remain inside another canonical subject. Promote to a character file only when the individual becomes independently referable and connected.

## Plot contamination test

Flag:

- chapter or act labels;
- encounter order;
- protagonist designation;
- prescribed revelation timing;
- quest steps;
- climactic confrontation;
- resolved character arc;
- claims about what the audience will learn in a scene.

Rewrite these as current world states, past facts, uncertain plans, relationships, or open pressures.

## Exit evidence

An important agent is deep enough when they can act intelligibly within the world, affect and be constrained by other subjects, and remain usable by many possible stories without SILK deciding one.
