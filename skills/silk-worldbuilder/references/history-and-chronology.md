# History and chronology lens

## History is transformation

Do not build a sequence of named events merely to imply age. History is the uneven process by which present subjects acquired their current form.

## Temporal model

Decide whether time is linear, cyclical, layered, disputed, locally measured, interrupted, timeless in parts, or otherwise world-specific. Distinguish event order, date certainty, duration, and cultural periodization.

## Era construction

Create an era when several load-bearing conditions differ from the periods around it. An era needs characteristic possibilities, constraints, institutions, distributions, and understandings, not only a ruler list.

Different societies may divide history differently. The authorial chronology may preserve uncertainty.

## Event depth

For a major transformation, inspect:

- preconditions and long pressures;
- actors and asymmetric knowledge;
- contingency and alternatives that were plausible at the time;
- immediate outcomes;
- adaptations and counteractions;
- later reinterpretation and myth;
- material, legal, linguistic, ecological, demographic, and institutional residues as relevant;
- who benefits from the dominant account;
- unresolved present pressure.

Do not force every event to affect every domain.

## Parallel histories

Track simultaneous developments and uneven contact. One civilization's foundational era may be another region's quiet continuity. Avoid a single global timeline in which every place changes when the central polity changes.

## Historical causality

Preserve accidents, path dependence, failed reforms, incomplete adoption, forgotten causes, and institutions surviving their original purpose. Do not make the present a perfectly optimized consequence of the past.

## Pressure state

Classify important historical consequences as resolved, institutionalized, remembered, misremembered, latent, recurring, declining, or actively destabilizing. Not every ancient conflict, seal, prophecy, dynasty, or catastrophe should reactivate in the present.

The present gains depth from settled layers and ordinary continuity as well as active pressure. Avoid arranging the entire world at one campaign-start crisis point.

## Memory and evidence

Separate event, surviving evidence, official history, local memory, religious interpretation, propaganda, and modern scholarship. Use the claim registry for load-bearing disputes.

## Chronology view

Generate an optional chronology view for navigation. It is an index, not a replacement for canonical subjects. Link each entry to subjects and present residues.

## Exit evidence

History is deep enough when current conditions can be traced through transformations, multiple regions and perspectives have temporal agency, important residues are visible, and chronology does not collapse into a decorative date list.
